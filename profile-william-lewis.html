William John "Uncle Billy" Lewis

~1834 – April 9, 1900
Des Arc, Iron County, Missouri
Compiled by Andrea Harter (née Rayfield), 2026 · Part of the Rayfield–Robinson Family History Project
Last updated: June 2026
Who was "Uncle Billy"? The Iron County Register called him "Uncle Billy" — but this wasn't a family nickname. In the rural Ozarks, "Uncle" was a title of community respect given to older men who had earned the regard of their neighbors. William John Lewis was one of those men. His obituary called him "an old and highly respected citizen... upright man and good citizen." He was Katie Lewis Robinson's father, making him great-grandfather to Opal Robinson Rayfield — and a direct ancestor of our family.

Abigail Johnson Lewis — The Wife Who Held It All Together

Abigail Johnson was born February 4, 1840, in Floyd County (or Pike County), Kentucky. She married William on October 30, 1857, in Iron County — she was just 17 years old. Census records consistently note she could not read or write — not unusual for the time and place, and no reflection of what she was capable of. She raised nine or ten children, managed a household through war and hardship, and kept the family together when it mattered most.

The 1900 census — taken just months after William’s death — lists Abigail as head of household, age 60, occupation: “Farmer.” She remained on the property until her death on Christmas Day, 1919, at age 79 — nineteen years after William. Whether she was actively working the land that entire time or gradually scaling back, we don’t know. What we do know is that she never left.

Confederate Military Service

When the Civil War reached the Missouri Ozarks, it wasn't about distant politics — it was about neighbors, brothers, and survival. William John Lewis enlisted on October 4, 1862, at Pocahontas, Arkansas, joining Company A of the 15th Missouri Cavalry under Colonel Timothy Reeves. He was about 28 years old.

He wasn't alone. His brothers John, Andrew, and Benjamin all enrolled in the same regiment on December 22, 1862. Four Lewis brothers, same unit. That tells you something about the pressures — and loyalties — of Iron County families during the war.

About the "Bond and Oath" — October 31, 1863 William signed a Union Bond and Oath of Allegiance at Pilot Knob on October 31, 1863. This is sometimes misread in Ancestry trees as a voluntary Union enlistment. It was not. This came after his arrest — it was the price of release, not a change of heart. Many Confederate-aligned men in the border regions signed these oaths under duress. The muster rolls confirm he returned to Confederate service: by January–February 1864, he was listed as temporarily attached to Kitchen's Regiment, recruiting.

Benjamin Lewis and the Wilson's Massacre

This is one of the most devastating chapters in the Lewis family story — and one of the best-documented.

Benjamin Lewis, William's brother, enlisted in the same regiment on December 22, 1862. Just over a year later, on Christmas Day 1863, Benjamin was captured at what history remembers as Wilson's Massacre — a Union attack on Confederate forces in the Iron County area. Benjamin was taken prisoner and shipped to Gratiot Street Prison in St. Louis, arriving December 31, 1863.

Gratiot Street Prison was a nightmare. A converted warehouse on Gratiot Street between Sixth and Seventh Streets, it held Confederate prisoners, political detainees, and accused guerrillas in overcrowded, disease-ridden conditions. Benjamin didn't survive it. He died on January 30, 1864 — exactly one month after arriving. He was buried at Jefferson Barracks National Cemetery, where his headstone reads:

Naoma's death and the orphaned children

The tragedy didn't end with Benjamin. Family tradition — passed down through multiple descendants — holds that his wife, Naoma, was killed while trying to bring food to prisoners at Gratiot Street Prison in St. Louis. The exact circumstances aren't recorded. Whether she knew Benjamin was already gone is something we'll never know.

Naoma's death date has not been established from any primary document, and her burial place is unknown. She does not appear in Mountain View Cemetery in Des Arc, where so many of her kin eventually came to rest. If she died in or near St. Louis, she may lie in one of the city's now-lost pauper burial grounds — Sublette Cemetery (the city's potter's field before the war), Koch Hospital Cemetery (where indigent and disease victims were buried during the Civil War era), or another unmarked site. Finding her remains one of the open questions in this family's story.

Benjamin and Naoma's children were now orphans. What happened next tells you everything about this family. The surviving Lewis siblings didn't send those children to an orphanage or let the county decide their fate. They divided the children among themselves and raised them as their own:

Land

William secured his family's future early. On September 10, 1859, he received two land patents — both cash purchases at $1.25 per acre under the Land Act of 1820. Together, they totaled approximately 160 acres in Iron County.

Common error on Ancestry trees: These patents are sometimes labeled as Homestead Act claims. They were not. The Homestead Act wasn't passed until 1862. William's patents were straightforward cash purchases under the 1820 law — you paid $1.25 an acre, you got your land. No residency requirement, no improvement clause. He was 25 years old with land of his own, two years before the war started.

Later Life and Death

Sometime in the mid-1880s, William suffered a disabling fall from a horse. According to his obituary, he was an invalid for the last 14 to 15 years of his life. That means from roughly his early fifties until his death, he couldn't work the land he'd bought as a young man. Abigail and the older children must have carried the load.

William died on April 9, 1900, in Des Arc. He was 65 years old. The Iron County Register noted his passing, calling him "Uncle Billy" — that community title of respect — and describing him as "an old and highly respected citizen" who was "an upright man and good citizen." He was buried at Mountain View Cemetery in Des Arc.

His headstone reads simply:
