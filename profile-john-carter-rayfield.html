John Carter Rayfield Sr

December 16, 1816 – July 27, 1903
Reynolds County, Missouri
Compiled by Andrea Harter (née Rayfield), 2026 · Part of the Rayfield–Robinson Family History Project
Last updated: June 2026
The Patriarch John Carter Rayfield Sr is the earliest Rayfield we can follow in real detail — census by census, land patent by land patent — from his childhood in Kentucky through nearly nine decades of life on the Missouri frontier. He is the anchor of our Rayfield line, the man who turned wilderness into a homestead and raised fourteen children across two marriages. His story is featured in Chapter 1 of The Rayfields — A Family History.

His Story

John Carter Rayfield arrived in Missouri in 1829 as a thirteen-year-old orphan. Both his parents were dead — his mother, a Mansfield woman whose first name has not been established, had died young, and his father James was gone by the time John Carter was old enough to travel. He made the journey west from Cumberland County, Kentucky, with his Uncle William, then in his forties, and Uncle John, who was younger and already married with a small child. They were headed for the wild Black River country in what would become Reynolds County — timbered Ozark hills, clear springs, and land that was still nearly open. John Carter would never leave.

By 1830 he was in Uncle William's household, learning the country. By the late 1830s he was married to Mary Ann "Polly" Mann, daughter of local Reynolds County settlers, and beginning the long work of building a homestead from scratch. He secured his first federal land patent in 1853 — forty acres — and a second in 1856: two hundred acres on Sinking Creek, both signed by President Franklin Pierce. He built two log cabins on that land and raised eight children with Polly, naming his sons after the heroes of the young republic: Andrew Jackson, George Washington, Benjamin Franklin. When Polly died in August 1860, leaving children ranging from a toddler to a teenager, he kept going. Five years later he married Nancy Ann Mann — Polly's first cousin, who already knew the children — and had six more. Fourteen children in all, across two marriages.

John Carter Rayfield spent seventy-four years on the same ground in Reynolds County. Seven decades of census records place him in the same valley, farming the same land, watching his children and grandchildren spread out from the homestead he'd built as a young man. When he died on July 27, 1903, at eighty-six years old, he had outlasted the frontier era, a Civil War, and most of the men he'd known since boyhood.

One thing he carried his whole life was a story about his ancestors — that his grandfather had come from England, settled in Virginia, freed his bondsmen, and rescued abandoned enslaved people. He told it to his grandchildren; they wrote it down. When researchers later checked the records, the core of the story turned out to be real. The man John Carter called his grandfather was actually his great-great-grandfather, William Rayfield Sr., who had arrived in colonial North Carolina from England in 1703 — a documented landowner and will-maker in Pasquotank Precinct. The family had carried a real history in its head for two hundred years.

Name Variations in Records

The Rayfield surname has been spelled many ways across three centuries of records:

Rayfield Raifield Rafield Raffiel Raffield Raphael

First name also appears as:

John Rayfield John Raifield John C Rayfield J. C. R. Rayfield John Carter Rayfield

The earliest colonial records (1703) use "Rayfield." The 1790 census spells it "Raifield." Census takers in Missouri used "Rayfield" and occasionally "Raifield." The Y-DNA haplogroup project uses "Raffield-Rayfield." All the same family.
