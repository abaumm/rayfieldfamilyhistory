Family Profile Des Arc July 15, 2026

The Salesman from Des Arc

Absalom Parker Robinson spent fifty years of his adult life fully documented — and the first twenty with almost no trace at all.

By Andrea Harter

Absalom Parker Robinson shows up in the record cleanly, over and over, for more than fifty years — five federal censuses, two marriage records, a death certificate, a newspaper mention, a headstone.1 Every one of those records, from 1880 to his death in 1936, lists the same birthplace: Missouri.2 But nothing tells us exactly when he was born — the records put it somewhere around 1856 to 1858 — or exactly where in Missouri, or who his parents were.

What we don't know

Absalom's biological parents have not been identified. Family oral history, passed down through a descendant of his brother Alexander, holds that his parents were from Tennessee and died when he was young, and that the children were separated and raised by different families.3 That's a family story, not a document — it may be right in its shape and uncertain in its particulars, and it's presented here as oral tradition, not as an established fact.

What the records do show is a household: the 1860 census for Arcadia, Iron County, Missouri, lists Isaac Robinson with a child named "Apsa," age five, born in Missouri.4 The name and age line up well enough with Absalom that this is very likely the same boy — though the census doesn't state a relationship for him the way it does for children clearly recorded as sons or daughters, so the family connection to Isaac Robinson is not spelled out in that record itself.

Whether Isaac Robinson was Absalom's guardian or his biological father is still an open question. DNA matches connecting to Isaac's other documented children — including daughters from an earlier marriage, unrelated to Absalom's mother whoever she was — point toward Isaac as a biological ancestor rather than only the man who raised him.5 One honest complication: Absalom's own line down to today runs through a later marriage into the Lewis family, so it's fair to ask whether some of that shared DNA could be coming from the Lewis side instead of the Robinson side. Checking the matches' own family trees directly hasn't turned up any Lewis ancestry so far — but most of those trees are quite shallow, so that check is reassuring, not conclusive. The honest answer is: probable, not proven. Isaac Robinson most likely was Absalom's biological father — but that conclusion rests on indirect DNA evidence, not a document that says so outright. (Isaac himself has his own tangled story — three wives across three states, maybe four, and an online-tree marriage that turns out to belong to a different man entirely. See Three Wives, and a Nancy Who Wasn't.)

A path to a firmer answer: Y-DNA

The DNA evidence above is autosomal — the kind everyone's ancestry test gives them — and it can point strongly toward a relationship without ever quite proving it. There's a more direct test available, if the right person exists to take it: Y-DNA, which passes essentially unchanged from father to son to son, tracing one unbroken male line back for generations, with none of the mixing that makes autosomal matches hard to untangle — including the Lewis-family question noted above, which a Y-DNA result would sidestep entirely, since it only ever reflects the strict father-to-son line, never anything from a mother's side. If a living man descends from Absalom through an unbroken chain of Robinson sons — still carrying the Robinson surname today — his Y-DNA could be compared against a similarly unbroken male-line descendant of one of Isaac Robinson's other sons, Volentine, David, or John, all documented alongside Absalom in the 1850 and 1860 censuses above. A match between the two would be about as close to definitive proof of shared paternity as genealogy gets. If you're a Robinson man who traces your line back through Absalom and would be willing to test, I'd love to hear from you.

Absalom grew up in Iron County and, as a young man, moved to St. Francois County, where on March 22, 1878, he married Josephine Parks at a Justice of the Peace's home in Perry Township.6 Their son, John Wesley Robinson — the future blacksmith of Des Arc — was born two years later.7 Over the next two decades Absalom and Josephine had thirteen children by the 1900 census's count; nine were still living that year, four had died as infants.8 Sometime between that 1900 census, where Josephine still appears at his side, and May 1904, when Absalom married again — this time to Mary E. Burcham, née Davis, in Iron County — Josephine's life ended.9 No death record for her has been found. Her death date and burial place remain unconfirmed.

For his first twenty years in the record, Absalom worked as a farm laborer, then a day laborer.1 Sometime between 1900 and 1910, that changed: the 1910 census lists his occupation as "salesman, sewing machine," and it stayed that way for the rest of his working life.1 In April 1929, a local newspaper's "Peola News" column caught him mid-round, at around seventy-one years old: "A. B. Robinson, the Singer Sewing Machine Salesman, was on our creek last Friday."10 It's a small line, but it's the clearest glimpse the records give of who he was to his neighbors — a familiar face still working his territory in his seventies.

Absalom died on June 17, 1936, at the Doniphan home of his son Delbert.15 He had been ill since March 1, 1936, with lobar pneumonia; his physician was Dr. Clifton Go Fort, and the death certificate's informant, "D. L. Robinson," was Delbert.11 By December 1935, he was already ill enough that both Delbert, from Doniphan, and his son J. W. Robinson, from Sullivan, came to check on him — so his time in Doniphan predates his final illness by several months, though exactly when he moved there isn't yet pinned down.16 He was returned to Des Arc for burial, with funeral services at Pentecost Church conducted by Rev. Nolan, and was laid to rest two days later at Mountain View Cemetery, where his headstone reads "A B Robinson."1512 He was survived by his wife Mary, who subsequently moved to St. Louis.15

A name, tangled with a stranger's

For years, online family trees conflated Absalom with a different man of nearly the same name and age — an Absolom Robinson who was born and lived his entire life in Hawkins County, Tennessee. A WikiTree profile and a FamilySearch record still carry that Tennessee man's parents attached to Absalom's name.13 The two men can be told apart on place alone: our Absalom's own records place him consistently in Missouri from 1880 onward, while the Tennessee man's 1870 census record places him, that same decade, still in Hawkins County.14 It's a reminder that a shared name and a roughly matching age are never enough to merge two people — place and an independent record trail have to line up too.

One more thread from the family's oral history, unresolved and undocumented: a story, passed down through Alexander's descendants, that as adults the separated brothers crossed paths again — that Absalom, suspecting a stranger might be the brother he'd lost as a child, asked to see his foot for a scar he remembered, and found it there.3 Whatever happened between the two boys who were split apart sometime before 1860, the record doesn't say. The story is family memory, not a document, and it's offered here as exactly that.
