John Carter Rayfield Sr

December 16, 1816 – July 27, 1903
Reynolds County, Missouri
Compiled by Andrea Harter (née Rayfield), 2026 · Part of the Rayfield–Robinson Family History Project
Last updated: June 2026
The Patriarch John Carter Rayfield Sr is the earliest Rayfield we can follow in real detail — census by census, land patent by land patent — from his childhood in Kentucky through nearly nine decades of life on the Missouri frontier. He is the anchor of our Rayfield line, the man who turned wilderness into a homestead and raised fourteen children across two marriages. His story is featured in Chapter 1 of The Rayfields — A Family History.

Name Variations in Records

The Rayfield surname has been spelled many ways across three centuries of records:

Rayfield Raifield Rafield Raffiel Raffield Raphael

First name also appears as:

John Rayfield John Raifield John C Rayfield J. C. R. Rayfield John Carter Rayfield

The earliest colonial records (1703) use "Rayfield." The 1790 census spells it "Raifield." Census takers in Missouri used "Rayfield" and occasionally "Raifield." The Y-DNA haplogroup project uses "Raffield-Rayfield." All the same family.

The Parentage Question

Here is one of those genealogy puzzles where you have to work backward from what can't be true to land on what probably is. No single document says "James Rayfield was John Carter's father." But the evidence keeps pointing that direction, and nothing contradicts it.

John Carter had two uncles who made the trip to Missouri with him: William Rayfield and John Rayfield. We know William was his uncle, not his father, because William was still alive in the 1850s, patenting land at age 71 — and family memory consistently called him "Uncle William," never grandfather. William's 1857 land grant places him firmly as a contemporary of the older generation.

That leaves James Rayfield (b. ~1793), who was gone by the time thirteen-year-old John Carter headed west in 1829. Both of John Carter's parents were dead by then — his mother Sarah Mansfield had died young (possibly in childbirth), and his father was gone too. Eddy Pratt's 2014 genealogical reconstruction reached the same conclusion about James being the father through an independent analysis of the same evidence.

A Note on the Mansfield Connection The story that Sarah Mansfield's family disinherited her for marrying a Rayfield is oral tradition, passed down through the generations. We haven't found the document that proves it. But the Mansfields were indeed slaveholders in Cumberland County, Kentucky, and a marriage to a family of modest means like the Rayfields would have been considered a poor match. The disinheritance story fits the social reality of the time, even if we can't yet pin it to a specific record.

The Journey West (1829)

Picture this: it's 1829, and a thirteen-year-old boy named John Carter Rayfield is leaving the only home he has ever known. Both his parents are gone. He is traveling west from Cumberland County, Kentucky with his Uncle William (about 43 years old) and his Uncle John (about 30, married to Nancy King, carrying their infant son Billy).

They were headed for Missouri — specifically, the Black River country in what was then Washington County. The land was wild, timbered, full of game and springs and possibility. It wouldn't become Reynolds County until 1845, when the Missouri legislature carved it out of the larger county.

By 1830, we find young John Carter listed in Uncle William's household on the census — Black River Township, Washington County (now Reynolds County), Missouri. He made it.

Marriage and Family

First Marriage: Polly Mann (~1837–1860)

John Carter married Mary Ann "Polly" Mann around 1837, when he was about twenty-one and she was about seventeen. Polly was the daughter of Arnold Mann and Lucinda Biggers — local Reynolds County families. Together they had eight children over eighteen years.

Polly died on August 1, 1860, at about age forty. The cause isn't recorded, but with her youngest child Permelie only three years old, the household was full of children ranging from toddler to teenager. John Carter was left a widower at forty-three with eight children to raise on the frontier.

Second Marriage: Nancy Ann Mann (1865–1902)

John Carter married Nancy Ann Mann formally in 1865, though their first child predates the marriage. Nancy was Polly's first cousin — which wasn't unusual on the frontier, where families lived close together and a widower with eight children needed help, and a woman who already knew the children was a natural choice. Nancy and John Carter had six more children together, bringing the total to fourteen.

Nancy died in 1902, one year before John Carter himself passed.

Notice the names: Andrew Jackson, George Washington, Benjamin Franklin. John Carter was naming his sons after the heroes of the Republic. These weren't random choices — they tell you something about how this frontier farmer saw himself and his country.

Pattern: Seven decades in the same county. John Carter Rayfield arrived in what would become Reynolds County as a boy of thirteen in 1829 and never left. He died there in 1903 at age eighty-six. That kind of rootedness tells its own story.

The Family Story

What John Carter Told His Grandchildren John Carter Rayfield told his grandchildren that his grandfather came from England, settled in Virginia, freed his bondsmen, and rescued abandoned enslaved people. This story was passed down through the generations and written up in the Vickery genealogy sketch.

This is one of those family stories that turns out to be mostly right in a way that makes the hair on the back of your neck stand up — once you dig into the records.

The "grandfather" John Carter described was almost certainly William Rayfield Sr, who was actually his great-great-grandfather, five generations back. That kind of compression is completely normal in oral tradition — a story gets shortened to "my grandfather" even when the actual relationship is more distant. What matters is the core facts he preserved:

  • "Came from England" — Confirmed. William Rayfield Sr (b. ~1670, England) arrived in the Albemarle region of colonial North Carolina in 1703.
  • "Settled in Virginia" — This is actually more accurate than it first appears. William settled in Pasquotank Precinct, North Carolina, but that area sat right on the disputed Virginia–North Carolina border. Before the 1728 Dividing Line survey, many residents weren't sure which colony they lived in. William may well have considered himself a Virginian.
  • "Freed his bondsmen" — We have not yet found the document confirming this, but it is a specific and unusual claim that would be strange to invent.

John Carter was carrying a real history in his head — compressed, simplified, but anchored in events that actually happened two hundred years before he was born. That is remarkable.
