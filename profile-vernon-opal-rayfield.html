Vernon Jack Rayfield & Opal Myrtle Robinson Rayfield

Vernon: 1909–1977 · Opal: 1911–2002
Iron County & Reynolds County, Missouri → St. Louis → Piedmont
Compiled by Andrea Harter (née Rayfield), 2026 · Part of the Rayfield–Robinson Family History Project
Last updated: June 2026
Opal Robinson Rayfield — The Keeper of Stories Opal was the family’s storyteller — and its archivist. In 1976, she sat down and wrote “A Tribute to My Parents in This Bicentennial Year Because They Left Their Mark” — a loving, detailed account of growing up in the Robinson household at Des Arc. But the tribute was just one piece of what she preserved. She clipped obituaries and newspaper articles about family, kept handwritten notes from her own genealogy research, and saved her mother Katie’s Bible with its handwritten records of births, deaths, and marriages. She had a framed family tree that hung in her dining room for years — unfortunately lost to extended family and time, and not yet tracked down. In her later years, she passed along mementos and antiques to family members as a way of sharing memories of earlier times and making sure the things that mattered didn’t disappear. That instinct to hold on, to write it down, to hand it forward — it’s the reason so much of this project was possible.

Their Story

Vernon Jack Rayfield was born September 9, 1909, the son of Franklin Rayfield and Myrtle Seal — raised in the same Reynolds and Iron County hill country where Rayfields had farmed for three generations. Opal Myrtle Robinson was born two years later, on June 11, 1911, in Des Arc — the daughter of John Wesley Robinson the blacksmith and his wife Katie Lewis. Both families had roots in the same small communities along the Black River. When Vernon and Opal married on January 27, 1928, in Annapolis, Iron County, Vernon was eighteen and Opal was sixteen. Neither had reached twenty-one, so their mothers — Myrtle Rayfield and Katie Robinson — came in person to give their consent at the ceremony.

Days after the wedding, the newlyweds were in a car collision near Annapolis while heading to church. They went on to have four children: Charlene, born in Des Arc in 1928; Shirley, born in Reynolds County in 1931; Michael, born in St. Louis in 1949; and Jack, born in St. Louis in 1957. The gap in those birth years tells the story of a family in motion. By 1933, Vernon and Opal were living on North 23rd Street in St. Louis, where Vernon worked as a chauffeur. During at least part of the early 1940s they pulled back toward the Ozarks — Vernon's 1941 draft registration lists his employer as J.W. Robinson and Son, his father-in-law's sawmill operation in Steelville — and they eventually settled in Piedmont, Wayne County. Vernon died there on November 9, 1977, at sixty-eight, and was buried at Mountain View Cemetery in Des Arc.

Opal lived to ninety-one, dying in Bridgeton, Missouri, on July 23, 2002. She was, in the truest sense, the family's historian. In 1976, as the nation celebrated its bicentennial, she sat down and wrote a tribute to her parents — a detailed, loving account of growing up in the Robinson household at Des Arc. She preserved her mother Katie's Bible, clipped obituaries, saved handwritten genealogy notes, and made sure the things that mattered didn't disappear. Without Opal's tribute, whole chapters of this family history would be dates and census entries and nothing more. She gave them life. She is buried beside Vernon at Mountain View Cemetery.
