John Wesley Robinson & Katherine "Katie" Lewis Robinson

The Blacksmith of Des Arc and the Woman Who Kept It All Together
Des Arc & Steelville, Missouri
Compiled by Andrea Harter (née Rayfield), 2026 · Part of the Rayfield–Robinson Family History Project
Last updated: June 2026
About This Profile John Wesley Robinson was the blacksmith, grocer, truck driver, and sawmill operator who kept his family going through every turn the Ozarks threw at him. Katie Lewis Robinson was the daughter of William John "Uncle Billy" Lewis of Annapolis, and a lifelong member of the Des Arc Missionary Baptist Church. Together, they raised seven children — a son and six daughters — in the small communities of Iron and Crawford Counties, Missouri. Their granddaughter Opal Robinson Rayfield is the richest source for their story, and her words appear throughout this profile.

Irma Maxine — A Loss That Shaped the Family Irma was just thirteen years old when she died of laryngeal diphtheria on January 20, 1934. A tracheotomy was performed, but it was not enough. Her father, J.W. Robinson, was listed as the informant on the death certificate — meaning he was the one who had to stand in the office and answer the questions. This was a man who had built caskets for other families' losses. Now the loss was his own.

The Blacksmith Shop

In Des Arc, John Wesley Robinson ran a blacksmith shop that served the whole community. He wasn't a specialist — he was the kind of blacksmith who could make whatever people needed, because there was nobody else to do it.

"He was a general blacksmith because everything he made was, as the saying is today, from scratch." — Opal Robinson Rayfield, 1976 tribute article (ARN0000046)

He made horseshoes, wagon wheels, entire wagons. He shaped the iron over the forge and put things together that people depended on every day. But there was another part of the work that tells you something about how small-town life really operated in the Ozarks: there was no funeral home in Des Arc. When someone died, J.W. built the casket.

"Every casket he made was his own idea and finished out with a high satin and lace." — Opal Robinson Rayfield, 1976 tribute article (ARN0000046)

Think about what that means. This was a blacksmith — a man who worked with fire and iron all day — and when a neighbor lost someone, he turned around and lined a casket with satin and lace. Each one made from his own design. That tells you everything about what it meant to be the person people counted on in a place like Des Arc.

The Grocery Store

At some point, J.W. added a grocery store to his operation. Why? Opal put it simply:

"He added a grocery store — since he had six girls, plenty of help." — Opal Robinson Rayfield, 1976 tribute article (ARN0000046)

The store ran on a barter economy, which was how most of rural Missouri worked at the time. People brought in eggs, berries, chickens — whatever they had — and exchanged them for the goods they needed. Cash was scarce. Trust wasn't.

On February 2, 1928, the Iron County Register reported that J.W. Robinson had sold his blacksmith shop to Charles Jones but would continue running his mercantile business. The forge was done, but the store kept going.

The Move to Steelville & the Sawmill

By the 1930 Census, J.W. was listed as a truck driver, and the family had moved to Steelville in Crawford County. The blacksmith shop was behind him, the grocery store was behind him, and now he was hauling loads and working timber.

He and his son Walter started a sawmill together: J.W. Robinson and Son. Father and son, cutting lumber in the Ozark hills. After J.W.'s death, Walter kept the sawmill running on his own.

Sawmill operation in the Steelville area. J.W. Robinson and son Walter L. Robinson.

J.W.'s Last Letter

On October 27, 1941, John Wesley wrote to his half-brother William from Steelville. He had just come home from nine days in a St. Louis hospital. We don't know what the diagnosis was, but we know he was not well. Two months later — on December 27, 1941, twenty days after the bombing of Pearl Harbor — he was gone.

He was 61 years old.

Katie After J.W.

After J.W.'s death, Walter continued running the sawmill alone. Katie eventually returned to Des Arc — back to the community where she and J.W. had raised their family, run the shop, run the store. But she didn't stay there forever. She moved to Pine Lawn, in St. Louis County, to live with her daughter Opal and son-in-law Vernon Rayfield.

She died there on May 18, 1946, at Opal's home.

After her death, Walter organized the auction of her personal property:

"Personal property of the late Mrs. J.W. Robinson, at her home in Des Arc, Mo." Organized by Walter L. Robinson.

WWI Draft Registration

On September 12, 1918, John Wesley registered for the draft in Iron County, Missouri. Serial No. 7161. He was 38 years old. There is no record of him being called to serve.
