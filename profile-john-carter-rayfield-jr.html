John Carter Rayfield Jr.

~1848 – ~1915
Reynolds County, Missouri
Compiled by Andrea Harter (née Rayfield), 2026 · Part of the Rayfield–Robinson Family History Project
Last updated: June 2026
The Bridge Generation John Carter Rayfield Jr. is the crucial link between the patriarch and the modern family. He was born on his father’s Reynolds County homestead, grew up on Sinking Creek, and spent his entire life farming the same Ozark hill country his father had settled. His son Franklin Peter Rayfield — born in that household in 1884 — is the subject of Chapter 2 of The Rayfields — A Family History. This profile also covers Jr.’s wife, Nancy Ann Farris, daughter of one of Reynolds County’s most significant nineteenth-century figures.

Name in Records

John Carter Rayfield Jr. appears in records almost always as a variant of his father's name, which can create confusion:

John C Rayfield J.C.R. Rayfield John Rayfield Carter Rayfield

His wife’s 1931 obituary calls him “Carter Rayfield” — the informal name that distinguished him from his father. The 1900 census lists the head of household as “J.C.R. Rayfield,” age 52. His wife goes by “Nannie” in the same record, though she is Nancy Ann Farris in her own records and obituary.

Nancy Ann's Father: Lucian Nester Farris (Context)

Nancy Ann’s father, Lucian Nester Farris (October 12, 1820 – August 8, 1876), was a Reynolds County farmer, two-term state legislator, and Confederate officer who became one of the county’s most prominent nineteenth-century figures. He arrived in what was then Ripley County (now Reynolds County) around 1840 and spent the rest of his life there. He died at fifty-five and was buried at Buffington Cemetery — the same ground where the Rayfields would eventually rest.

Nancy Ann was from Lucian’s first marriage, to Nancy Hyatt (married February 15, 1840), who died around 1854 when Nancy Ann was seven. Lucian remarried in 1856, bringing stepmother Anna Carter into the household.

A full biographical profile of Lucian Nester Farris is planned separately. Sources for this summary: ARN0000233 Farris biography; ARN0000234–ARN0000235 Confederate service records.

The Connection Between the Families

Lucian Farris and John Carter Rayfield Sr. were exact contemporaries in Reynolds County — Lucian arrived around 1840, John Carter Sr. was there from 1829. They would have known each other: both farmers, both in the same river valley community, both eventually buried at Buffington Cemetery. When John Carter Jr. married Nancy Ann Farris in the late 1860s, he was knitting together two of the county’s founding families.

That Nancy Ann’s funeral in 1931 was held at the Buffington schoolhouse — not a church in town, not a funeral parlor — says something about where this family rooted itself. Even sixty years after the original settlement, the Buffington community was still their home ground.

Research Gap: Full Obituary Text The May 2026 research session identified the existence and key details of Nancy Ann Farris Rayfield’s 1931 obituary (born June 12, 1847; died February 8, 1931; five sons named with locations; three unnamed daughters; husband preceded her ~16 years; funeral at Buffington schoolhouse). However, the full obituary text — including the sons’ given names and their locations — has not yet been retrieved and transcribed. Locating this obituary (likely Wayne County Journal-Banner, February 1931) would resolve multiple open questions at once.

Research Notes

On the death date ~1915: This is an estimate derived from a statement in Nancy Ann’s obituary. It is probable, not proven. A direct death record (death certificate, probate filing, or obituary for JCR Jr. himself) has not been located. Missouri began statewide death registration in 1910, so a death certificate may exist if he died after that year. Reynolds County death records should be searched for the 1910–1920 range.

On the Farris connection: The connection between “Nancy Ann Farris” in the 1847–1931 obituary and “Nannie” in the 1900 census (wife of J.C.R. Rayfield, age 52, consistent with Nancy Ann born 1847) is well-supported by converging evidence: matching birth years across multiple census records, the Farris family’s documented presence in Reynolds County with a daughter of the right age and name, and family records confirmed by the Franklin Rayfield profile. Confidence: Probable.

On the 1870 census woman: The 1870 census (ARN0000180) shows John Rayfield, 22, in Logan Township with a woman of approximately matching age. This is consistent with a recent marriage to Nancy Ann (born 1847, age 22–23 in 1870), but the 1870 census does not have a relationship column. The identification of the woman as Nancy Ann is probable based on age, location, and the fact that she appears in later records as his wife. Confirmation via a marriage record would be stronger proof.
